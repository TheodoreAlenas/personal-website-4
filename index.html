  6. Preferences

    Generally I prefer social teams. It's true that nerdy teams are friendly and more open and inclusive like a family, they're not for me however. I'm totally fine with social people who make fun of me for being weird, I also like talking to older people or energetic people who make noise sometimes and I'm open to different ethnicities and interests, but nerds bring me bad memories. I've been the only person of the team that didn't pass the course, twice. I've been ghosted and they came to me hours before the deadline for help, twice. These are four different people. I've done UI user tests and they dismissed them, generally when I offer some way to save up time people don't take it and that drives my nerves. I haven't had such problems with social people. I suppose I can work with a nerdy team again and I'll get used to it over time, but if there's a more social team I'd go for it first.

    Ideally, I'd like a team that practices test driven development or has some other way to make it easy to try out the code and take it apart. It's not necessary, but I've written code that has convenient tests and I'd like to write such code as a job. I wrote fast tests for the KeyMouth project for example, with the one letter at a time messages, and they were fast enough that I could run them without much thought. There are some tests that try wiring the backend with a half set up front end, which are slow tests, but for the pagination, for translating the input stream to a data structure similar to the UI, for responding to empty messages, there are tests that don't wire things up and I just run them all every time I make a small change. I don't write tests in many projects but when I do it makes them fun.

    Finally, I prefer working in person in an office. There are benefits to working from home but in my internship at Helvia, when we met in the office, I could tell when it's a good time to throw a question. A messaging app tells you if the other person is on a call but from up close you can tell if they're focused, tired or relaxed. Also, in person, it's very easy to peek at another's screen. This can be both good and bad, but I've opened fun discussions from looking at people's screens. The most important benefit though is that you can say something in a word or two as someone is talking to you. When I message someone to ask if they see something obviously wrong about my work, they are tempted to give a long answer and in person you can make something good out of that, but in messages it's trickier. So I prefer working close to the others.
